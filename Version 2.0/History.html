Founders

Virginia State University was founded on March 6th, 1882. The legislature passed a bill to charter the Virginia Normal and Collegiate Institute, this bill was sponsored by Delegate Alfred W. Harris. Harris was an American legislator in Virginia. He also served four two-year terms in the Virginia House of Delegates from 1881 until 1888. The first class consisted of 126 black students and seven faculty members on a $20,000 budget. In 1920, Virginia Institute transformed into a land-grant institution providing readily available, research-based programs and educational resources to improve their community. In 1979 the legislature passed a law that changed the official name of the college to Virginia State University.

Colson Hall

Colson Hall is named after the late James M. Colson, Jr. Colson was born October 15th, 1855 in Petersburg, Virginia. "After attending public school he completed his preparation for College at the high school of Middleboro, Mass. His work in college helped him gain his membership in Phi Beta Kappa". After graduation, he was offered a position as the chair of science at Virginia Normal and Collegiate Institute. James was the first science teacher and second president of Virginia Normal and Collegiate Institute. After twenty-one years, Colson resigned and accepted a position as a principal at John A. Dix Industrial School in Dinwiddie, Virginia. James M. Colson died May 22nd, 1909 in Petersburg, Virginia.

Foster Hall

Foster Hall is named after the late Dr. Luther H. Foster Jr. Dr. Luther H. Foster JR. was born March 21st, 1913. Foster earned his undergraduate degree from Virginia State College in 1932 and Hampton University in 1934. He then received his master's degree in business administration from Harvard University in 1936 and a master's degree from the University of Chicago in 1941. Foster earned his Ph.D. from the University of Chicago in 1951. Foster served as the fourth president of the Tuskegee Institute in Alabama from 1953 to 1981. He was also inducted into the Alabama Academy of Honor, and was awarded the Star of Africa from Liberia. Dr. Luther H. Foster, Sr. severed as president until he died July 6th, 1949.

Owens Hall

Owens Hall is named after the late George Washington Owens. Owens was the son of former slaves who migrated to Kansas in the early 1870s to find free land, his family settled in Wabaunsee County. George Washington Owens was the first African-American to graduate from Kansas State College, after graduating he developed the Department of Agriculture at Virginia Normal and Industrial Institute in 1908. "Owens wrote the constitution and by-laws for new farmers of America, which joined the National FFA Organization in 1965". In 1945, Owens retired from Virginia State College as the chairman of the Department of Agriculture. In Honor of his service Owens Hall was named after him in recognition of 25 years of his service at Virginia State College.

Hunter-McDaniel

Hunter-McDaniel is named after the late Dr. Reuben McDaniel, Sr. McDaniel was born July 27th, 1902 in Fairfax, Virginia. "In 1924 he was awarded a scholarship to study at Rutgers University in New Brunswick, New Jersey. Here at Rutgers, he majored in mathematics and physics. He was then inducted into Phi Beta Kappa during his junior year and graduated in June 1928 with a Bachelor of Science degree. After graduating he was offered a position as a mathematics and physics professor at Shaw University in Raleigh, North Carolina. McDaniel taught at Shaw University until February 1931, until he was offered another position as a professor of mathematics at Virginia State College in Petersburg, Virginia."In 1949, McDaniel was appointed as dean at Virginia State College, then as Director of the School of Arts and Sciences. He continued to serve at Virginia State College until he retired after serving 20 years as dean. Dr. Reuben McDaniel, Sr died January 19th, 1975."

Gandy Hall

Gandy Hall is named after the late John M. Gandy. He was born in Mississippi in 1870, the son of sharecroppers. He received his high school diploma from what is now Jackson State University. Gandy attended various schools such as Fisk University Wesleyan, Colgate, and Oberlin. Due to a lack of funding, Gandy had trouble with attendance despite this Gandy eventually earned his Ph.D. He then began teaching at Virginia Agricultural and Normal College which is now Virginia State University. Gandy was an educator, an African-American activist, and the third president of Virginia College. "In 1916 Gandy led a committee that proposed a monument to be dedicated at Jamestown to the First Africans in Virginia that landed in 1619". Activism was rare for African-American college presidents of state-funded schools during Jim Crow segregation, but with his courage and leadership skills, he inspired many people to advocate for African- American history. Gandy died in 1947 four years after his retirement.
